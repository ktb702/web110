Accessibility

Have you ever tried to access a webpage, say from your mobile, and the page wouldn’t load correctly? Or the links wouldn’t work? Or you had to scroll all the way over to read all of the text? Or even worse you couldn’t read some of the text at all? It’s very frustrating not being able to access certain sites. Imagine if there were limiting factors, say for the visually impaired? According to the World Health Organization, “Globally, at least 2.2 billion people have a vision impairment or blindness”¹. And that’s only looking at one disability. There are other limiting factors to internet access such as hearing impairment, limited mobility (such as the inability to use a mouse), photosensitivity, and cognitive disabilities.

Internet users without disabilities also benefit from accessibility. Accessibility affects how we view websites on different devices (mobile, smart tv, tablets, etc). It also affects people situationally, such as when you might have slow internet connection, limited bandwidth, bright lights, or cannot listen to audio in a certain setting. Jared Smith and his team at WebAIM surveyed 1792 screen reader users and found that 10.9% of respondents used assistive technologies even though they didn’t have a need for it².

Companies stand to benefit from making websites accessible also. Without doing so they stand to lose some of their potential audiences as well as market share. Accessibility improves user experience and therefore customer satisfaction. There are also legal ramifications for not following accessibility guidelines.

With all the benefits of accessibility, one might think most websites have already gone that way. Sadly, according to the hostingtribunal blog, “a whopping 70% of websites do not meet the basic principles outlined in the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG)”³. The good news is that there are more tools available for creating accessible websites than ever before. The W3 has a great Accessibility Evaluation Tools List to get started with.
